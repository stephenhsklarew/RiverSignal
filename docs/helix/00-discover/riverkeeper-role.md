# The Riverkeeper Role

*A one-page introduction for early-adopter Riverkeepers.*

## What a Riverkeeper is

A **Riverkeeper** is a trusted local steward of a single watershed inside RiverPath.
Where everyone else *reads* a river's story, a Riverkeeper *tends* it — keeping the
photos, descriptions, and narrative for their watershed accurate, local, and alive.
Think of it as the difference between a national field guide and a knowledgeable
local who actually walks the bank.

Each Riverkeeper is assigned to one (or a few) watersheds they know firsthand. They
don't run the whole platform — they curate *their* river. Above all, the Riverkeeper is
**accountable for the quality and accuracy of everything shown for their assigned
watershed**: the photos are real and current, the species are right, the conditions
make sense, and the story is true. They are the quality bar for their river.

## What a Riverkeeper can do that others can't

Regular visitors can browse a watershed and contribute their own photo observations.
A Riverkeeper, through the `/admin` area, can shape what *everyone* sees for their
watershed:

| Capability | Visitor | Riverkeeper |
|---|:---:|:---:|
| View the river's page, species, conditions, story | ✅ | ✅ |
| Submit their own photo observations | ✅ | ✅ |
| **Set the watershed's splash photo + tagline + description** | — | ✅ |
| **Edit the watershed's River Story** (and refresh its narration) | — | ✅ |
| **Curate the "Fish Present" photos** for the watershed | — | ✅ |
| **Curate the "What Fish Are Eating Now" insect/prey photos** | — | ✅ |

Every change a Riverkeeper makes is **scoped to their watershed**, takes effect on the
live `/path` experience, and is **audit-logged** — so edits are traceable and reversible.

## Why it matters

- **A single, accountable quality bar per watershed.** Someone who knows the river is
  responsible for making sure its content is right — and is the first to catch a wrong
  species, a stale photo, or an off narrative. Quality stops being everyone's job (and
  therefore no one's) and becomes one trusted person's.
- **Local truth beats generic defaults.** A stock photo and an AI-drafted blurb get a
  watershed *most* of the way there. A Riverkeeper closes the gap with the real fish,
  the real hatch, the real photo from the real bank.
- **Story over schema.** People don't return for data tables — they return for the
  story of *their* river, told by someone who knows it. Riverkeepers are how that story
  stays true.
- **It scales trust without a central bottleneck.** Instead of one team trying to keep
  dozens of watersheds current, the people closest to each river keep their own — which
  is the only way this gets to hundreds of watersheds.
- **It honors the contributor.** Observations already keep their original photographer
  and privacy. The Riverkeeper role extends that respect to the *whole* watershed page.

## What's coming next

Content curation is the starting point. The role is designed to grow toward true
watershed stewardship — on the roadmap (not yet built):

- Verify the **fish-present** and **stocking** lists for the watershed
- Curate **local fishing & hatch** guidance and **access / recreation** notes
- **Moderate** community observations and flag bad data
- Post **stewardship updates** (restoration days, closures, safety notices)
- A lightweight **Riverkeeper dashboard** for their watershed's health and activity

## Getting started

Riverkeeper access is invitation-based during early adoption. Once granted, you'll sign
in and find your watershed's content tools in the `/admin` area — pick your watershed,
and its tabs (Splash Card, River Story, Fish Photos, Insect Photos) are yours to tend.
No technical setup required; changes go live on the next page load.

> *Early-adopter note:* you're helping define this role. What you wish you could change
> about your river's page — but can't yet — is exactly the feedback that shapes what
> Riverkeepers can do next.
